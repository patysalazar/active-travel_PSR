# Active Travel and Moderate-to-Vigorous Physical Activity Across Four Canadian Cities: A Longitudinal Cohort Analysis of the INTERACT Study

This repository contains code used for the manuscript:

**Active Travel and Moderate-to-Vigorous Physical Activity Across Four Canadian Cities: A Longitudinal Cohort Analysis of the INTERACT Study**

**Authors:** Patricia Salazar-Ramirez, Daniel Fuller, Meghan Winters, Ehab Diab, Naomi (Seula) Lee, Yan Kestens 

### Abstract 
**Background:** Active travel has been shown to increase physical activity levels; however, less is known about whether this relationship persists over time across different urban contexts and the role of the built environment in this association. The aim of this study was to examine the longitudinal association between active travel and moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA) among adults in four Canadian cities and to assess whether walkability, transit access, or household income modify this relationship.
**Methods:** We analysed data from the INTerventions, Research and Action in Cities Team (INTERACT) cohort, a pan-Canadian longitudinal study conducted in Victoria, Vancouver, Saskatoon and Montréal between 2017 and 2024. Walkability and transit access were assigned using Canadian Active Living Environments measures linked to participants’ residential dissemination areas. Linear mixed-effects models with participant-level random intercepts estimated associations between active travel and weekly MVPA, adjusting for age, gender, city, ethnicity, household income, walkability, and transit access. Interaction terms assessed effect modification.
**Results:** The analytic dataset included 4,165 participants contributing 6,834 observations. Median weekly MVPA was 300 minutes, and most observations reported regular active travel. In adjusted mixed-effects models, occasional active travel was associated with 65 additional minutes of MVPA per week (95% CI: 8.5, 122), while regular active travel was associated with 175 additional minutes per week (95% CI: 121, 229), compared with no active travel. Walkability, transit access, and household income did not show clear evidence of effect modification. 
**Conclusions:** Active travel was positively associated with weekly MVPA across four Canadian cities and repeated survey waves. The lack of observed modification by walkability, transit access, or income suggests that the positive association between active travel and MVPA was consistent across the built environment and income categories in this cohort, while also highlighting the need for more urban planning efforts aimed at making active mobility safe, convenient, and accessible.

### Data Availability

The data used in this study are not publicly available due to participant privacy, ethics, and data governance requirements. Approved INTERACT users may request access through the appropriate INTERACT data access and ethics approval processes.
